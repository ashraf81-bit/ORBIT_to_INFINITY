MISSION CLASS — LAUNCH VEHICLES

Launch Systems

Every journey to space begins with fire.

Launch systems are the backbone of space exploration. They generate the immense thrust required to overcome Earth's gravity and deliver payloads into orbit and beyond.

To leave the ground is to defy physics with engineering. Every launch is a controlled explosion — millions of kilograms of force directed with millimeter precision, over the span of minutes, to place tonnes of hardware exactly where it needs to be.

Escape velocity from Earth is 11.2 km/s. No chemical reaction, no mechanical force, no natural phenomenon achieves this without a rocket. Fire is not optional. It is the only way.
Escape Velocity 11.2 km/s
Typical Burn Time 8–12 Minutes

Staging Events 2–3 Stages
CLASS — 001 / EARLY ROCKETS

The First Leap

The journey began with simple yet powerful machines. Early rockets proved that reaching space was possible, marking the beginning of human exploration beyond Earth's atmosphere.

The V-2, born from wartime engineering, became the first man-made object to cross the Kármán line. Within a decade, its lineage had evolved into the rockets that carried astronauts to the Moon.

V-2 Thrust 56,000 lbf
Fuel Ethanol / LOX
First Space Crossing 1944
Heritage Foundation of all modern rockets
V-2 — first object to reach space
Saturn I — bridge to lunar missions
Proved orbital insertion was achievable
CLASS — 002 / HEAVY LIFT

Giants of Ascent

These massive rockets are designed to carry enormous payloads into orbit and beyond. They played a crucial role in missions to the Moon and continue to support deep space exploration.

The Saturn V remains the most powerful rocket ever flown — 7.6 million pounds of thrust at ignition. Its roar physically shook the ground miles away. NASA's Space Launch System carries that legacy forward.

CLASS — 003 / REUSABLE

Redefining the Journey

Modern rockets are no longer single-use. Reusable systems dramatically reduce costs and allow rapid access to space, revolutionizing the economics of space travel entirely.

The Falcon 9's booster landing — a 70-metre column of steel descending vertically on a column of fire — was dismissed as impossible before SpaceX made it routine. Starship aims to make the entire stack reusable.

Falcon 9 Thrust 1.7 Million lbf
Booster Landings 300+
Cost Reduction ~10× vs. Expendable
Turnaround ~24 Hours
Falcon 9 — world's most flown orbital rocket
Starship — fully reusable super-heavy
Vertical propulsive landing technology
CLASS — 004 / DEEP SPACE VEHICLES

Beyond Earth's Pull

These rockets are built for missions that go far beyond Earth orbit — carrying probes, telescopes, and explorers into deep space. They combine raw power with extraordinary reliability.

The Atlas V has launched some of humanity's most ambitious missions — New Horizons to Pluto, Curiosity to Mars, the Parker Solar Probe toward the Sun. Each flight was flawless. Zero failures in over 80 launches.

Atlas V Thrust 860,000 lbf
Fuel Liquid Hydrogen / RP-1
Success Rate 100% (Atlas V)
Payload to GTO 8,900 kg
Atlas V — 100% mission success rate
Delta IV Heavy — triple-core architecture
Missions: Pluto, Mars, Sun, outer planets
CLASS — 005 / FUTURE SYSTEMS

The Next Frontier

Future launch systems aim to be faster, more efficient, and capable of interplanetary travel. The next generation of propulsion will compress years into months, and months into weeks.

Nuclear thermal rockets — long theorized, now in active development — could cut travel time to Mars in half. Combined with fully reusable architectures, they represent the most radical shift in propulsion since the V-2.

Starship's fully reusable architecture targets a cost of $10/kg to orbit — down from $54,500/kg on the Space Shuttle. If achieved, access to space becomes as economical as air freight.
