MISSION CLASS — ORBITAL INFRASTRUCTURE

Space Observatories & Orbital Habitats

In orbit, we build eyes to see the universe — and homes to exist within it.

From powerful telescopes that capture light from distant galaxies to space stations where humans live and work, this domain represents the fusion of exploration and habitation.

Orbiting Earth, these structures exist in a threshold between worlds — permanently falling yet never landing, constantly in motion yet precisely placed.

The Hubble Space Telescope has made over 1.5 million observations. The ISS has hosted over 270 individuals from 21 nations. Both orbit at roughly 400 km — inaccessible to all but a few hundred humans in history.
Part I — Space Telescopes
OBSERVATORY — 001 / HUBBLE

The Eye That Changed Everything

Launched in 1990, Hubble transformed our understanding of the universe. It captured breathtaking images of galaxies, nebulae, and distant stars — revealing the vast scale and beauty of the cosmos in unprecedented detail.

Its early imagery was blurred by a flawed mirror — corrected by a servicing mission in 1993. That repair turned a failed instrument into the most productive telescope in history.

OBSERVATORY — 002 / JAMES WEBB

Seeing the Beginning of Time

The James Webb Space Telescope observes the universe in infrared, allowing it to see through cosmic dust and capture light from the earliest galaxies formed just hundreds of millions of years after the Big Bang.

Positioned at the L2 Lagrange point — 1.5 million kilometers from Earth — it sits in permanent shadow, cold enough to detect light from galaxies formed when the universe was less than 4% of its current age.

OBSERVATORY — 003 / NEXT GENERATION

Next Generation Vision

Future telescopes aim to detect Earth-like planets, analyze their atmospheres for biosignatures, and search for signs of life beyond our solar system. The next generation will answer questions we haven't yet learned to ask.

Concepts include segmented mirrors spanning tens of meters and space-based arrays capable of directly imaging exoplanet surfaces — technology that makes JWST look like a first draft.

The Habitable Worlds Observatory, currently in planning, is designed with a single purpose: to find and characterize Earth-like planets around nearby stars — and to determine, for the first time, whether we are alone.

Part II — Orbital Habitats
HABITAT — 001 / INTERNATIONAL SPACE STATION

A Home Above Earth

The International Space Station is a habitable satellite where astronauts live and work in microgravity. It serves as a laboratory for scientific research and a symbol of international cooperation unlike anything else in history.

Since November 2000, it has been continuously inhabited — the longest continuous human presence beyond Earth's surface. Sixteen nations contributed to its construction across more than 40 assembly flights.

HABITAT — 002 / LIFE IN ORBIT

Living Beyond Gravity

Astronauts aboard the ISS conduct experiments, maintain systems, and adapt to life in zero gravity — floating, working, and sleeping in space, tethered to walls to keep from drifting.

Every daily act becomes a problem to solve: water collected as floating globules, food eaten from sealed pouches, exercise performed two hours daily to prevent bone and muscle loss that microgravity accelerates silently.

From the ISS, astronauts witness 16 sunrises every 24 hours — each one a four-minute arc of fire across a curved horizon, repeated across a lifetime compressed into a single mission.
