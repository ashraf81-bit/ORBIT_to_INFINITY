Orbital Systems

Satellites

Above us, silent machines map, guide, and connect the world.

Satellites are not just objects in orbit — they are the backbone of modern civilization, enabling communication, navigation, weather prediction, and scientific discovery.

Thousands of them circle our planet right now, each locked in precise trajectories, each performing a silent role that the world below has come to depend upon completely.

From geostationary giants that hold a fixed gaze over entire continents, to low-orbit constellations weaving a blanket of connectivity — satellites have redefined what it means to know, communicate, and navigate.
Type 01 — Communication

Global Voices in Orbit

These satellites relay signals across continents, enabling television broadcasts, internet connectivity, and international communication. Positioned mostly in geostationary orbit, they remain fixed relative to Earth, acting as constant communication hubs.

A single geostationary satellite can cover one-third of Earth's surface. Together, networks like Starlink weave low-orbit constellations that deliver broadband to the most remote corners of the planet.

Without communication satellites, GPS would be silent, mobile roaming would vanish, and live global news coverage would be impossible.
Intelsat Starlink SES Astra Viasat
Type 02 — Navigation

Precision Beyond Sight

Navigation satellites form global positioning systems that allow devices to determine location with incredible accuracy. By transmitting synchronized signals from multiple satellites, they enable real-time positioning anywhere on Earth.

The principle is elegant: four satellites, each broadcasting its position and a precise timestamp, allow a receiver to triangulate its exact coordinates anywhere on the globe — within meters.

Modern GPS has become so embedded in civilian life that aviation, shipping, logistics, and emergency response now depend on it entirely. Remove it, and global infrastructure halts.
GPS (USA) GLONASS (Russia) Galileo (EU) BeiDou (China)
Type 03 — Weather

Eyes on the Atmosphere

Weather satellites monitor cloud formations, storms, and climate patterns. They provide critical data for forecasting, disaster warning, and environmental monitoring, helping humanity prepare for nature's extremes.

Infrared and visible imaging sensors capture the planet's atmospheric dynamics in real time — from the spiral arms of a hurricane to the invisible movement of jet streams thousands of kilometers wide.

A single GOES satellite continuously monitors an entire hemisphere, transmitting live imagery every 10 minutes — the clearest window humanity has ever had into the planet's weather engine.
GOES-16 Meteosat Himawari NOAA-20
Type 04 — Scientific

Exploring the Unknown

Designed for research, these satellites observe space, study Earth, and gather data beyond human reach. From cosmic radiation to deep space imaging, they expand our understanding of the universe.

Above the distortion of Earth's atmosphere, space telescopes capture light that has traveled billions of years — revealing the earliest galaxies, newborn stars, and the true scale of the cosmos.

The James Webb Space Telescope peers back over 13.6 billion years to observe the universe's first light. It is humanity's deepest eye — orbiting 1.5 million kilometers from Earth.
Hubble James Webb Chandra X-Ray Terra
Type 05 — Surveillance

Invisible Guardians

Operating mostly in secrecy, these satellites are used for reconnaissance, intelligence gathering, and national security. They monitor activities on Earth with high-resolution imaging and advanced sensors.

From detecting missile launches to tracking troop movements in near real time, surveillance satellites have fundamentally changed the nature of geopolitical intelligence — making nothing on the surface truly invisible.

Resolution so fine that commercial equivalents can read a newspaper from orbit. Classification so deep that most nations deny specific capabilities exist.

"Watching everything. Seen by no one."

KH-series (USA) Lacrosse SAR Ofek (Israel)
