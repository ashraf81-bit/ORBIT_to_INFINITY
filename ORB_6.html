MISSION CLASS — DEEP SPACE EXPLORATION

Deep Space Probes

Beyond the reach of Earth, our machines carry the story of humanity into the infinite.

Deep space probes are robotic explorers sent far beyond Earth's orbit. They travel across planets, moons, and even into interstellar space, gathering data from regions humans cannot yet reach.

These machines are not merely tools — they are messengers. Each one carries instruments, cameras, and sometimes a deliberate record of our existence, cast into the void on the off chance that something, somewhere, might one day find it.

Five spacecraft have achieved escape velocity from our solar system. None will reach another star for tens of thousands of years. Yet they travel — and we listen.
PROBE — 001 / VOYAGER 1

The Farthest Traveler

Launched in 1977, Voyager 1 is the most distant human-made object. It has crossed the heliopause — the boundary of our solar system — and now travels through interstellar space, carrying a golden record intended for any intelligent life it may encounter.

Its radio signal, traveling at the speed of light, takes over 22 hours to reach Earth. Yet the data still comes — a whisper from 23 billion kilometers away.

PROBE — 002 / VOYAGER 2

The Grand Tour Pioneer

Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have visited Uranus and Neptune. It provided humanity's first detailed images of these distant ice giants and continues its journey into interstellar space alongside its twin.

Its trajectory — the Grand Tour — was only possible due to a rare planetary alignment that occurs once every 176 years. Engineers seized it with extraordinary precision.

Distance from Earth 19.9+ Billion km
Status ● Active
Signal Delay ~18.5 Hours
Planets Visited Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune

Now in interstellar space
PROBE — 003 / NEW HORIZONS

Beyond Pluto

New Horizons delivered the first close-up images of Pluto in 2015, revealing a complex and geologically active world — complete with mountains of water ice and a vast nitrogen plain. Nothing we expected was there. Everything that was there, we hadn't imagined.

It continues deeper into the Kuiper Belt, exploring ancient frozen remnants of the solar system's formation more than four billion years ago.

Distance from Earth 8.7+ Billion km
Status ● Active
Pluto Flyby July 2015
Speed ~14 km/s
First close-up of Pluto
Kuiper Belt object exploration
Fastest spacecraft at launch
PROBE — 004 / PIONEER 10

The First Messenger

Pioneer 10 was the first spacecraft to travel through the asteroid belt and make direct observations of Jupiter. Launched in 1972, it paved the way for every outer solar system mission that followed.

Attached to its frame is a gold-anodized plaque — a message to the cosmos. On it: the figures of a man and woman, the location of our sun, and the structure of hydrogen. A letter in a bottle, thrown into an infinite ocean.

Distance from Earth ~15 Billion km
Status ● Silent
Last Signal 2003
Launch Year 1972
First asteroid belt crossing
First Jupiter direct observations
Pioneer plaque — humanity's first message
PROBE — 005 / PARKER SOLAR PROBE

Touching the Sun

Unlike every other probe that travels outward into the cold dark, the Parker Solar Probe dives inward — hurtling toward the Sun at over 690,000 km/h. It has become the fastest human-made object ever built.

Its heat shield withstands temperatures exceeding 1,370°C while its instruments remain cool, studying the solar corona and solar wind — phenomena that affect every planet in the solar system, including Earth.

Closest Approach 6.1 Million km
Status ● Active
Max Speed 692,000 km/h

Closest approach to the Sun ever achieved
Fastest human-made object
Extreme heat shielding technology
