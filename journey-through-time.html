1957

First Touch of the Sky

Sputnik 1

Launched on October 4, 1957 by the Soviet Union.

Weight: 83.6 kg (184 lbs); diameter: 58 cm (23 inches)

First man-made object to orbit Earth.

Transmitted radio signals that could be received by radios worldwide.

Sparked the space race between the USA and USSR, leading to major investments in science and technology.

Demonstrated the feasibility of satellites for communication and observation.

1961

First Human Among the Stars

One small step for man, one giant leap for humanity

On April 12, 1961, Yuri Gagarin became the first human to orbit Earth aboard Vostok 1. Suspended in the black void, circling our planet every 89 minutes, Gagarin's flight proved that humans could survive the extreme conditions of space. The world watched in awe as he radioed back his famous words: "Poyekhali!" ("Let's go!"), sparking hope that humans could one day explore worlds beyond our own.

1969

One Giant Leap

Apollo 11 — Moon Landing

On July 20, 1969, NASA's Apollo 11 mission landed humans on the Moon for the first time.

Neil Armstrong became the first human to walk on the lunar surface, followed by Buzz Aldrin.

Armstrong's words — "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind" — echoed across the world.

600 million people watched the event live on television, making it one of history's most witnessed moments.

1971

A Home in Orbit

Salyut 1 — First Space Station

The Soviet Union launched Salyut 1 on April 19, 1971 — the world's first space station.

It orbited Earth at about 200 km altitude and hosted the crew of Soyuz 11 for 23 days.

Proved that humans could live and work in space for extended periods.

Laid the foundation for future stations including Mir and the International Space Station.

×
1981

Reusable Wings

Space Shuttle Columbia — STS-1

On April 12, 1981, Space Shuttle Columbia launched on the first orbital test flight.

It was the first reusable crewed spacecraft in history, revolutionizing how humans access space.

The Shuttle program flew 135 missions over 30 years, deploying satellites, building the ISS, and servicing the Hubble Telescope.

Carried crews of up to 8 astronauts and enabled unprecedented scientific research in orbit.

×
1998

House of Nations

International Space Station — Assembly Begins

The first module of the ISS, Zarya, launched on November 20, 1998, beginning construction of humanity's greatest collaborative space project.

A joint project of 15 nations, the ISS orbits at ~400 km altitude and travels at 28,000 km/h.

Has been continuously inhabited since November 2000 — over 270 people from 20 countries have lived aboard.

Serves as a laboratory, observatory, and staging point for future deep-space exploration.

×
2004

Rovers on Mars

Mars Exploration Rovers — Spirit & Opportunity

NASA's twin rovers landed on Mars in January 2004, designed to operate for 90 days.

Spirit operated for over 6 years; Opportunity for nearly 15 years — far exceeding expectations.

Discovered compelling evidence that liquid water once flowed on the Martian surface.

Traveled a combined distance of over 45 km across the Martian terrain, returning thousands of images.

×
2011

The Private Revolution

SpaceX Dragon — First Private Spacecraft to ISS

On May 22, 2012, SpaceX's Dragon became the first commercial spacecraft to berth with the ISS.

SpaceX's Falcon 9 made its first flight in 2010, beginning a new era of commercial launch services.

The shift opened access to space to private companies, dramatically reducing launch costs.

This commercialization sparked a global industry of satellite deployment, space tourism, and deep-space ambitions.

×
2020

Humans Return to Orbit

SpaceX Crew Dragon — Crew Demo-2

On May 30, 2020, SpaceX launched astronauts Bob Behnken and Doug Hurley to the ISS — the first crewed American spacecraft since the Shuttle's retirement.

Marked the beginning of commercial crew transportation as a routine service.

The capsule's reusability and cost savings represent a new paradigm for human spaceflight.

NASA's Commercial Crew Program has since enabled regular crewed missions to the ISS.

2025

Gateway to the Moon

Artemis Program — Return to the Moon

NASA's Artemis program aims to land the first woman and next man on the Moon, with ongoing missions building toward a sustained lunar presence.

The Lunar Gateway — a small space station in lunar orbit — is being assembled as a staging point.

International partners including ESA, JAXA, and CSA collaborate on this next chapter of human exploration.

Sets the stage for eventual crewed missions to Mars later this decade.

2026

Humanity's Next Frontier

Mars & Beyond — The New Space Age

By 2026, SpaceX's Starship — the largest rocket ever built — is targeting its first crewed orbital missions and uncrewed Mars cargo flights.

NASA's Artemis III aims to put humans back on the lunar surface for the first time since 1972.

Lunar mining and in-situ resource utilization experiments are planned to test technologies for permanent off-Earth habitation.

The next generation of space telescopes and interplanetary probes will redefine our understanding of the cosmos.

Humanity stands at the threshold of becoming a truly multi-planetary species.
